Summary Layers of the Earth
From Exosphere to Core
01
Exosphere
The outermost layer gradually merges with the vacuum of space. It consists of very thin gases and is where satellites and space debris orbit the Earth.
Thermosphere
02
This layer contains very few gas molecules and is where the International Space Station orbits. Temperatures here can reach thousands of degrees Celsius.
03
Mesosphere
The mesosphere is the third atmospheric layer, where temperatures decrease with increasing altitude. It's also where meteors burn up.
Stratosphere
04
The stratosphere is the atmospheric layer above the troposphere, contains the ozone layer, and absorbs harmful UV radiation.
05
Troposphere
The troposphere is Earth's lowest atmospheric layer, where weather occurs, and it extends up to 10 kilometers from the surface.
Crust
06
The Earth's outermost layer is the crust, which includes the continents and ocean floors. It's where most geological activity, like earthquakes and volcanic eruptions, occurs.
07
Lithosphere
The lithosphere is the rigid, outermost layer of the Earth. It includes the Earth's crust and a portion of the uppermost mantle. It's like the hard shell of our planet.
Asthenosphere
08
A semi-fluid layer beneath Earth's lithosphere, allowing tectonic plate movement. It's in a semi-molten state, which allows it to flow slowly over geological timescales.
09
Mantle
The mantle is a semi-solid layer that lies between the Earth's crust and core. Convection currents within the mantle drive the movement of tectonic plates.
Outer Core
10
The outer core is a liquid layer of Earth's core beneath the mantle, composed primarily of molten iron and nickel, responsible for generating the planet's magnetic field.
11
Inner Core
Earth's innermost layer, composed of solid iron and nickel at extremely high temperatures and pressures, plays a critical role in the generation of Earth's magnetic field.
